Contributing Factors to Your Body Type

  • Metabolism

    • Metabolism refers to all the chemical processes going on continuously inside your body that allow life and normal functioning (maintaining normal functioning in the body is called homeostasis). These processes include those that break down nutrients from our food, and those that build and repair our body.

    Metabolism regulates:

    • Breathing
    • Circulating blood
    • Digesting food
    • Growing and repairing cells.
    • Managing hormone levels
    • Regulating body temperature

    Your metabolic rate is influenced by many factors – including age, gender, muscle-to-fat ratio, amount of physical activity and hormone function.

    • Amount of body fat – fat cells are ‘sluggish’ and burn far fewer kilojoules than most other tissues and organs of the body.
    • Crash dieting, starving or fasting – eating too few kilojoules encourages the body to slow the metabolism to conserve energy. Metabolism can drop by up to 15% and if lean muscle tissue is also lost, this further reduces metabolism.
    • Age – metabolism slows with age due to loss of muscle tissue, but also due to hormonal and neurological changes.
    • Gender – generally, men have faster metabolisms because they tend to be larger.
    • Genetic predisposition – your metabolic rate may be partly decided by your genes.
    • Hormonal and nervous controls – metabolism is controlled by the nervous and hormonal systems. Hormonal imbalances can influence how quickly or slowly the body burns kilojoules.
    • Infection or illness – metabolism increases because the body has to work harder to build new tissues and to create an immune response.
    • Amount of physical activity – hard-working muscles need plenty of energy to burn. Regular exercise increases muscle mass and teaches the body to burn kilojoules at a faster rate, even when at rest.
  • Muscle Insertions

    • Muscle insertions are determined entirely by genetics. These insertions refer to the points where muscles attach to bones and act as anchor points during contraction. While muscle insertions generally don’t impact strength, they can significantly influence how a muscle appears.

    For example, a "disconnected chest" insertion might be considered less desirable compared to other chest insertions. On the other hand, aesthetics in areas like the biceps often come down to personal preference.

    It’s important to note that muscle insertions cannot be changed, no matter how much you train. However, what does make a difference is filling out your frame through consistent exercise. Building muscle and achieving a balanced physique is far more important than worrying about genetic features you can’t control.

  • Soreness

    • Many people believe that you must be sore the next day in order to indicate a good workout. However, this is not necessarily the case, especially for experienced lifters. You can still have a great workout and not be sore the next day.

    Soreness is NOT synonomous with muscle growth, and to certain extremes, can inhibit it. Excessive exercise can lead to rhabdomyolysis, or a breakdown of the muscles in your body. Remember, moderation is key.

  • Weight

    • Many people focus on the number on the scale, but it is not the best representation of body changes. The number on the scale is a factor of many things such as how much water you've drank, what you ate and what time you're weighing in.

    Often when a person loses a significant amount of weight, the number on the scale goes up because of building muscle from exercise.

    If you want to accurately track your weight loss and muscle development, we recommend recording measurements of arms, waist and thighs. And if you're going to use the scale, weigh in at the same time every day.
